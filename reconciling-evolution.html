Reconciling Evolution with Latter-day Saint Faith

A comprehensive exploration of how faith and science can harmoniously coexist

Introduction: Faith vs. Science or Faith and Science?

Can a faithful member of The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints accept the science of evolution? Some assume that belief in biblical creation automatically conflicts with modern evolutionary biology. However, many Latter-day Saint scholars and leaders have found ways to harmonize their faith with scientific findings. In fact, historian Ben Spackman answers the question "Can evolution be reconciled with the Bible?" with an emphatic yes‚Äî"but not the way people think". Rather than seeing science and scripture as bitter enemies, we can view them as answering different questions. This article explores how Latter-day Saints can believe in their religion and accept God working through scientific processes, such as evolution, by understanding scriptural interpretation and church teachings on the subject.

The Problem of "Concordism"

One key issue is concordism. Concordism is, as Spackman explains, "an assumption that science, especially evolution or the age of the earth ‚Ä¶ and scripture are speaking the same language. They're talking about the same thing and therefore they have to match up or one of them is false". In other words, many people presume that scripture and science must concord (agree perfectly) in their details about creation. If there's a mismatch‚Äîsay, between Genesis and geology‚Äîthen you must choose one or the other. This mindset leads to two common extremes: On one hand, some "young-earth" creationists insist that science must be twisted to fit a literal reading of the Bible (e.g. claiming the Earth is only a few thousand years old). On the other hand, some assume scripture must have secretly anticipated modern science, interpreting Genesis's days or wording in a metaphorical way to force a fit with the scientific record.

Think of it this way:

When the Psalmist says "the sun rises" (Psalm 104:22), we don't reject astronomy because we know the Earth rotates. Similarly, when Moses describes photosynthesis occurring before the sun appears (Genesis 1), it's perfectly fine - he's teaching theology, not botany.

Spackman argues that this underlying assumption is flawed. The belief that Genesis is providing a natural history of the earth, a physical history of the earth as a science textbook would is "simply not an accurate assumption". The Bible was never intended to give a modern scientific account of how God created the world. When we drop the expectation that scripture and science speak in the same terms, the supposed conflict between faith and evolution looks very different.

Interpreting Genesis in Context

The Bible reflects an ancient worldview‚Äîfor example, its writers assumed a flat, motionless earth beneath a solid dome-like sky. In antiquity, for millennia, people believed the Earth was flat, and indeed the Bible's text supports that view in passages that describe the "foundation" of the earth or the sky as a firmament. Today we know the earth is a sphere orbiting the sun‚Äîan idea once deemed "flatly contrary to scripture"! Yet accepting a round Earth and heliocentric solar system poses no crisis of faith for believers. This illustrates a key point: scripture often speaks in the terms of the worldview of its original audience. God did not reveal astronomy to ancient Israel; instead, He taught spiritual truths using their existing cosmic understanding. Likewise, the creation story in Genesis was given to teach theological lessons (about God's power, the purpose of life, the sacredness of the Sabbath, etc.), not to reveal scientific details of Earth's natural history.

Modern readers must therefore interpret Genesis in context rather than through a purely scientific lens. Spackman explains that Genesis 1 was "set against [a] contextual background that the Israelites knew" in their time. When we "fast forward 2,500 years" to today and start asking modern scientific questions about evolution, dinosaurs, or the age of the Earth, we are often "asking Genesis 1 to answer [questions] not within its scope whatsoever." The cosmology in Genesis is an ancient cosmology‚ÄîGod's message was couched in the scientific understanding of that era‚Äîmeant to teach important spiritual truths to ancient people, not to deliver a physics or biology lesson. As Spackman puts it, we are "deeply misreading Genesis when we read it through a scientific lens," whether that means insisting it matches science in a young-earth creationist way or claiming it cryptically predicts modern scientific discoveries. Both approaches are "deep misreadings" because they force Genesis into a role it was never meant to play.

Key Point:

"If I have a non-scientific creation account, but I try to read it as scientific history, that's not a literal reading. That's a misreading." - Ben Spackman

It may be surprising, but even the term "literal" reading needs clarification. Historically, to read scripture "literally" meant to read it "according to the author's intent", with full context of genre and background. A truly literal reading recognizes, for example, when a biblical passage is poetic or symbolic and treats it as such. By contrast, reading a text outside its intended genre or context can lead to misinterpretation. As Spackman notes, "If I have a non-[scientific] creation account, but I try to read [it] as [a] scientific history, that's not a literal reading. That's a misreading." In other words, Genesis can be 100% "true" doctrinally without being a 1:1 chronicle of material events in the way a science textbook would be. Latter-day Saints believe truth can come by both study and faith; understanding why scripture was written helps us discern how to read it. The purpose of Genesis is to teach that God is the Creator and that we are His children, created in His image‚Äînot to explain the precise biochemical or geological mechanisms God used to form life.

LDS Perspectives: Early Debates and Evolving Views

Understanding how to reconcile evolution and the gospel is not a new endeavor. During the early 20th century, there was significant discussion among Church leaders and thinkers about evolution. Notably, two scientist-apostles of that era ‚Äì Elder James E. Talmage and Elder John A. Widtsoe ‚Äì were quite open to evolution and saw scientific discoveries as reinforcing their faith. They viewed new scientific truths as evidence of God's work: the unfolding of natural laws by which the Lord governs the universe. In other words, scientific discovery for them was "evidence of God's use of natural laws" in creation. In contrast, other prominent figures, such as Elder (later President) Joseph Fielding Smith, took a very different stance. Joseph Fielding Smith felt that the scriptural Creation account could not accommodate evolution at all. He read Genesis in a very literal, concrete way and concluded that accepting human evolution (or an Earth billions of years old) would undermine faith. These differing viewpoints set the stage for a long-running conversation about science and religion within the LDS community.

By the 1920s and 1930s, this debate had become quite pronounced. Elder B. H. Roberts (a theologian and Seventy), along with Talmage and Widtsoe, argued that faith and evolution need not conflict, whereas Joseph Fielding Smith became the most vocal proponent of strict creationism. According to Spackman's research, those more science-friendly leaders "seemed to be very amenable to evolution," while Smith had an outsized influence on LDS thought in favor of creationism, to the point that he "temporarily won the evolutionary debate" within the Church culture. In the early 1930s, the First Presidency even intervened in a dispute between Church leaders over evolution. President Heber J. Grant issued guidance in 1931 that the Brethren should "leave Geology, Biology, Archaeology and Anthropology ‚Ä¶ to scientific research", rather than fight over those subjects, since none of those fields are central to the salvation of souls. In short, the Church as an institution chose a neutral stance: the details of Earth's physical development were not to be a doctrinal battleground.

Despite that official neutrality, Elder Joseph Fielding Smith continued to champion a creationist view in the mid-20th century. In 1954, after other proponents of evolution-friendly views had passed away (or been overshadowed), he published Man, His Origin and Destiny, a book condemning organic evolution and defending a young-earth, special-creation perspective. His influential status and prolific writings (he later became the 10th Church President) meant that for many Latter-day Saints, Smith's views became the default understanding of how to reconcile (or rather not reconcile) science and the Bible. Spackman wryly notes that Smith "waited until his opponents [like Roberts and Talmage] died" and then put out his book, which included material he had written decades earlier. Unmoved by the scientific expertise of some of his peers, Joseph Fielding Smith's priority was maintaining what he saw as fidelity to scripture, even if that meant dismissing mainstream science. In one sense, that unwavering commitment to scriptural truth is admirable. However, the crux of the issue is how one reads scripture. Spackman observes that Elder Smith exemplified "19th-century assumptions" inherited in Mormonism‚Äîa very literalist, absolutist approach‚Äîwhereas the more science-friendly LDS leaders were willing to interpret scripture in light of context and metaphor. Smith insisted his narrow reading of Genesis was the only acceptable one, which, as discussed, many scholars would call a misreading of Genesis's intent.

Despite the dominance of Joseph Fielding Smith's viewpoint for a time, other Church leaders quietly maintained a more open approach. Perhaps the most notable was President David O. McKay, who served as Church President from 1951 to 1970. President McKay was personally friendly toward scientific inquiry and evolutionary theory. On several occasions he indicated that belief in evolution was compatible with belief in God. For example, McKay once approvingly cited a scientist's teachings about conscience and evolution, implying that "Genesis in no way prohibits evolution" in the origins of human bodies. He did not see the theory of evolution as a threat to the gospel plan. In fact, an instructive episode occurred during McKay's presidency: a BYU professor prepared an article discussing DNA and evolution for an official Church magazine. President McKay not only gave it the green light, he read the entire article and called it "fantastic work," insisting that it be published with a special disclaimer up front. The printed disclaimer stated that the article was "not presented as an official position of the Church", which McKay wanted, since the Church had (and still has) no binding doctrine on evolution. But significantly, McKay was enthusiastic about sharing scientific findings with Church members. He clearly felt that honest science could be discussed openly among the Saints without undermining faith. This exemplifies an LDS leader seeing evolution as potentially part of God's creative process, even if the Church wouldn't state that as doctrine for everyone.

Past Leaders Lay the Foundation

The openness of these early leaders laid important groundwork. Today's Church culture increasingly embraces the idea that seeking truth through both study and faith strengthens rather than threatens testimony.

Current Understanding: God's Methods and Our Faith

In recent decades, the Church's stance on evolution has been consistently neutral-to-positive. There is no official Church doctrine declaring evolution false. In fact, the Church has explicitly stated that "the Church has no official position on the theory of evolution", calling it "a matter for scientific study". What the Church does affirm is why we are here and who we are in an eternal sense: we are children of God, created in His image, and He is the ultimate Creator of life. How God carried out the creative processes is left to be discovered through science and study. As one Church-approved statement put it, "the scriptures tell why man was created, but they do not tell how". That "how" is something we investigate with the tools of science, always under the premise that God set the natural laws in motion. Many faithful LDS scientists feel that evolution is one of those natural processes instituted by God. It is notable that at Church-owned schools like BYU, evolution is taught robustly in biology courses, and faculty (many of them devout Latter-day Saints) have created resources to help students reconcile these truths. The BYU Department of Biology's "Reconciling Evolution" initiative, for example, provides videos and curriculum showing that one can accept evolution and remain a committed believer. The underlying message is that all truth is part of the gospel, whether discovered through a microscope or through revelation.

Official Statement:

"The scriptures tell why man was created, but they do not tell how."

So, can you believe in both God and evolution? Yes. For Latter-day Saints, there is grandeur in the view of life evolving under God's direction. One can trust that God is the author of all truth. If something is scientifically true, it will not ultimately conflict with God's word‚Äîonly with human interpretations of God's word. Sometimes our understanding of scripture may need to expand (as with the Earth's place in the cosmos), and sometimes scientific theories are refined over time. But seeking knowledge by study and by faith is a sacred mandate. President Russell M. Nelson, himself a world-renowned surgeon, taught that "there is no conflict between science and religion. Conflict only arises from an incomplete knowledge of either science or religion (or both)." As we increase in light and knowledge, apparent conflicts can be resolved.

It is also important to recognize the different domains of science and religion. Science deals with the natural world‚Äîprocesses, evidence, and mechanisms‚Äîwhile religion deals with spiritual truths‚Äîmeaning, morality, and purpose. They answer different questions. For example, consider the biblical Flood of Noah. Scientifically, a global flood covering the highest mountains would leave immense geological evidence everywhere ‚Äì yet no such universal evidence is found. Does this mean the Bible is "false"? Not necessarily; it may mean we need to interpret the Flood story in its ancient context (perhaps as a local event or a symbolic teaching story) rather than insisting on a strictly global, literal reading. On the other hand, consider the Resurrection of Jesus Christ ‚Äì a miracle at the heart of Christianity. Science by its nature cannot prove or disprove such a singular supernatural event; it is a matter of faith and divine power. Understanding these differences helps us avoid stumbling when science and scripture seem to differ. We should neither reject scientific evidence out of hand nor toss aside sacred scripture. Instead, we seek a fuller understanding of both. As Spackman wisely cautions, "Some things [science can tell us], not everything, and it depends on the question." We must be careful ‚Äì "generalizing is dangerous and being simplistic is dangerous because things are rarely simplistic".

Concrete Example:

DNA mutations and natural selection can explain how human intelligence evolved over millions of years. This doesn't threaten the doctrine that we're created "in God's image" with divine potential - it just shows us one possible method God used to prepare our mortal bodies for eternal spirits.

Conclusion: "All Truth Is Part of the Gospel"

Latter-day Saints believe God is the Master of the universe, the architect of life. Whether He chose to form our bodies over eons through evolution or in an instant is ultimately secondary to the truth that we are His children with a divine destiny. The Gospel and evolution answer different aspects of that truth. The Gospel teaches who we are and why we are here. Evolutionary science (and other sciences) can enlighten us as to how God's creative processes might have unfolded over time. Far from being a threat to faith, understanding evolution can inspire even greater awe for the workings of God. As one BYU biologist wrote, studying evolution can be "faith affirming" and fill one with reverence for a God who set in motion such a beautiful, law-governed process. Indeed, many Latter-day Saints find that faith and science together provide a more expansive view of truth than either could alone. We can echo the words of BYU professor Duane Jeffery: "No evidence denies the hand of God in directing evolution."

In summary, an LDS member can confidently accept evolution in those "aspects" where strong evidence exists (such as the shared ancestry of living organisms, the age of the earth, natural selection, etc.) while still having faith in divine purpose and miracles. It requires us to interpret scripture with care, letting it teach us the deeper "why" rather than forcing it to answer scientific "how" questions it wasn't intended to. It also requires humility in science, acknowledging what science can and cannot determine. But this balanced approach is not only possible‚Äîit is already being lived by many devout scientists and leaders in the Church. Ultimately, all truth is compatible. In the words of Elder John A. Widtsoe, "truth is truth," whether from the bible or the biology lab, and "in the end, truth will prevail". The faithful can therefore study evolution or any other field without fear, trusting that God works through natural laws and that ongoing revelation and scientific discovery will both illuminate His handiwork. In this way, Latter-day Saints can love the gospel of Jesus Christ with all their hearts and still marvel at the grandeur of life's evolution, seeing in it the fingerprints of a loving Creator.

üåü Faith + Science in Action

Imagine a world where faithful Latter-day Saints lead in both spiritual understanding and scientific discovery. Where our children study DNA mutations in biology class without fear, knowing God works through natural laws. Where we see the Cambrian explosion's creativity as evidence of divine artistry, not a challenge to faith.

This isn't compromise - it's embracing the fullness of truth. God reveals truth through scripture and through the microscope. Both the revealed word and the discovered world testify of His reality and glory.
